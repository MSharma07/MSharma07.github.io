Introduction

Formula 1 is a sport where every second counts, and performance is shaped by a combination of driver skill, car engineering, and race-day strategy.

While final race positions are publicly availlable and celebrated, the underlying performance metrics that contribute to those outcomes are less often explored from a predictive standpoint. This project investigates the question:

Can we predict a driver's final race position using in-race performance data such as fastest lap speed, lap rank, and total race time?

Understanding the answer to this question holds significance for both racing teams and sports analysts.

By identifying which in-race metrics most strongly influence final results, teams can optimize their race strategies, allocate engineering resources effectively, and fine-tune driver performance. Additionally, this analysis contributes to the growing field of sports analytics, where data-driven models are increasingly being used to understand and improve competitive performance.

To address our question, we curated and explored historical Formula 1 race data, applying a range of statistical and machine learning methods. Specifically, we used correlation analysis to understand relationships between race metrics, outlier detection to examine variability in lap speeds, and hypothesis testing to compare performance across racing teams. In the primary analysis, we trained and evaluated multiple machine learning models to assess how well final race position could be predicted from key performance variables.

Our findings aim to provide both quantitative insights into Formula 1 race performance and practical implications for how data science can support strategic decision-making in competitive sports.

# convert the fastest lap time variable to a timedelta format

def convert_to_secs(lap_time):
  try:
    minutes, seconds = lap_time.split(':')
    total_seconds = float(minutes) * 60 + float(seconds)
    return pd.to_timedelta(total_seconds, unit='s')
  except:
    return np.nan

results['fastestLapTime'] = results['fastestLapTime'].apply(convert_to_secs)

  • Welchs t-test comparing fastest lap speeds between Ferrari and McLaren

For this method, I wanted to see if two of the top F1 teams Ferrari and McLaren had significantly different average fastest lap speeds. I used a Welchs t-test, which is a version of the t-test that works even if the two groups have different variances or sample sizes (which is usually the case in F1 race data).

After running the test, I got a p-value of 0.1162, which is more than 0.05, so we fail to reject the null hypothesis. This means we dont have enough evidence to say that the teams are statistically different in terms of their average fastest lap speeds.

The strip plot above also backs this up both teams have a pretty similar spread of lap speeds with a lot of overlap. There are no clear gaps or shifts that would suggest one team is consistently faster.

So based on this test and the visualization, theres no strong difference between Ferrari and McLaren in terms of fastest lap performance in the dataset.

Machine Learning Approach

Our exploratory data analysis identified several race performance metrics like the fastest lap speed, lap rank, and total race time as being correlated with final race position which will be included in our feature set. We also gained insights regarding the distributions of fastest lap speed.

Although our initial correlation matrix highlighted a few key metrics, other variables such as starting grid position, year of the race, or finishing status may also contribute predictive value. By starting with a broad set of features including those identified by the correlation matrix and using feature importance, we can identify which variables most strongly influence the driver's final race position.

After training and comparing several machine learning models, we found that ensemble models worked best for predicting a drivers final race position.

The Gradient Boosting Regressor gave the strongest performance overall, with an R score around 0.6. This means it was able to explain about 61% of the variation in final race position, which is pretty solid given how unpredictable racing can be. Its MAE and RMSE were also the lowest out of all the models, showing that it consistently made more accurate predictions compared to the others.

Random Forest also performed well, but slightly worse than Gradient Boosting, with an R around 0.64. This still shows that non-linear models are a much better fit for this kind of racing data than simple linear models.

Linear Regression, which we used as a baseline, gave an R of about 0.57. So, it did capture some of the patterns between features like fastest lap speed and race position, but clearly didnt handle the complexity of the data as well as the ensemble models.

Support Vector Regression (SVR) didnt perform well at all it barely explained any variation in the final positions (R around 0.006). This likely happened because SVR is very sensitive to hyperparameters and feature scaling, and in this case, the default settings werent a good match for our dataset. Because of this, we didnt bother tuning it further.

In summary, the results show that features like fastest lap time, lap rank, race time, and starting grid position definitely help explain race outcomes, but they dont tell the whole story. Race strategy, pit stops, weather conditions, and incidents like crashes are also huge factors, but they arent captured in this dataset. Still, its clear that a well-tuned ensemble model like Gradient Boosting can provide a meaningful predictive baseline for understanding Formula 1 race results.

Final Insights and Conclusions

In this project, we conducted an in-depth exploration into predicting Formula 1 drivers' final race positions based on various in-race performance metrics. Our analysis combined rigorous statistical methods with advanced machine learning techniques, providing both accurate predictive capabilities and meaningful insights into race dynamics.

Through our initial exploration, we saw some interesting connections between key performance factors. For instance, our Pearson correlation analysis showed a strong negative relationship between drivers' final positions and points earned (r = -0.58), and also between their fastest lap ranks and points earned (r = -0.55). On the other hand, we found a strong positive relationship between final positions and fastest lap ranks (r = 0.62), clearly indicating that drivers who consistently record faster laps tend to finish better. Surprisingly, we noticed that starting grid positions, although intuitively important, werent as strong predictors as actual race performance metrics like fastest lap rank or total race time. This means that a drivers performance during the race matters more than their starting spot.

Our boxplot analysis of fastest lap speeds also provided valuable insights by highlighting significant outliers. Most lap speeds clustered between 190220 km/h, but we observed extreme deviations as a result of mechanical problems, track incidents, or exceptional performances by particular drivers or teams. Additionally, when we used a Welch's t-test to compare Ferrari and McLaren, the two leading constructors, we found no significant difference in their average fastest lap speeds (p-value = 0.1162). This indicates how closely competitive these teams are at the top of Formula 1.

In our machine learning analysis, we tested several models including Linear Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Support Vector Regression. Gradient Boosting stood out as the most accurate, achieving an R score of about 0.607. This means it explained over 60% of the variability in race outcomes, accurately predicting drivers' finishing positions typically within just 12 spots. Additionally, our error analysis showed that mistakes made by this model were generally small, evenly distributed, and unbiased.

From a practical standpoint, our work offers Formula 1 teams actionable insights into areas they might focus on for strategic improvements. For instance, teams might achieve better results by focusing more on consistent in-race performance, like maintaining fast laps and effective pacing, rather than putting excessive emphasis on qualifying positions alone.

This project highlights a well-rounded data science approach, combining solid statistical analysis with reliable predictive modeling. We thoughtfully integrated multiple data sources, confirmed key relationships through correlation and significance testing, and fine-tuned our models to ensure the results were not only accurate but also meaningful and applicable beyond just our dataset.

Overall, our project successfully identified key factors influencing Formula 1 race outcomes, demonstrating the strength of data-driven methods in revealing meaningful patterns, even in dynamic, complex environments like motorsports. These insights can significantly enhance strategic decision-making and deepen understanding of Formula 1 dynamics for both professionals in the field and anyone interested in the sport.
